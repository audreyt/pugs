"Perl 6 Myths"


Version: 2 (8th German Perl Workshop, 2006)
Speaker: Juerd
Duration: any(5..10) minutes

This talk is about Perl 6, not about Parrot.

Perl 6 is an April fools joke

No, that is Parrot.

String concat will be underscore

No, it will be tilde.

Perl 6 is backwards compatible

No. Parrot can handle Perl 5, but Perl 6 itself is a new language. There will perhaps be a conversion script.

Perl 5 is good enough

Perl 5 has slow subs, unreadable internals, and no formal specification. Threading in Perl 5 is a problem.

Perl 6 development is closed and secret

Almost everything happens in public mailing lists. You can join today and hack away at Parrot, Pugs, and the many subprojects.

Perl 6 is harder than Perl 5

There is more to learn, but it will not get harder. Consistency (less exceptions!) and new ground breaking features make things easier. The end result, I think, is balanced.

Perl 6 is slower

Whoever says this, is lying or paranormally gifted...

Or... basing his claim on the current state of Parrot or Pugs, which are nice software but not quite ready for production, and no indication of the final product's performance.

CPAN-modules lose their value

Through PONIE, many Perl 5 modules will be usable in Parrot, and many popular modules will probably be rewritten.

And of course we get modules for free from languages that switch to Parrot.

There are 0 lines of code in Perl 6

Simply not true. If modern programming is test based, then our 35_000+ lines of Perl 6 tests (obviously, written in Perl 6), are a very good head start. Also, while maybe no lines of Pugs will be in the final implementation, the knowledge gained will.

Who measures in LoC, anyway?

Perl 6 depends on Parrot

Even though The Plan prescribes that Perl 6 will run on Parrot, and that Parrot is written primarily for Perl 6, we can have Perl 6 without Parrot. In fact, Pugs already does support multiple backends, like Javascript, PIR (Parrot), its own evaluator, and... Perl 5's vm.

The rest of the world makes the standard

Other languages often have good features, and we should steal them. However, the dot is not stolen because other languages use it. It is stolen because other languages use it and it is better. It saves two keystrokes (67%), one character (50%), and visual clutter.

The dot makes it Java-like

That's up to the users. I think we can make it Ruby-like, python-like, or Perl 6-like instead of Java-like.

Don't worry, we won't have system().foo().bar().i().value()
.please().increment();

$i++ will still be there.

Obfuscation and golf will die

Perl 6 has many new ways to obfuscate and shorten code. There's less flexibility in where you can put whitespace, so putting it in nice drawings becomes harder, but you get hyperoperators, junctions, and twigils to compensate. If you want to write unreadable, ugly code, you still can!

It'll take years until we have Perl 6

Perl 6 is already around, and you can play with it as much as you like. There's even an implementation, even though that isn't necessary the final official thing. Perhaps it will indeed take a few more years before Perl 6 is ready for production, but it will be released when it's ready.

If you want to help make it ready, please contribute!

It's hard to contribute

We try to make it easy. Committer bits are given out to everyone who wants them, there is a shared development machine you can get free access to, there are open mailinglists, there are IRC channels.

If you find it hard to contribute, that says something about you, not about Perl 6, IMnsHO.

Perl 6 is not Perl

It depends on your definition of Perl. You should consider basing your definition of Perl on Larry Wall's definition of Perl, which obviously includes Perl 6. TODO: My Perl 5 knowledge will be useless.
